# 09 — Risks & Open Questions

> The part most plans skip. This model is better than the commission marketplace — but "better" isn't "easy." Here are the real ways it still fails, and the questions to answer before betting a year on it.

## Top risks (ranked)

### 1. Chef willingness-to-pay is unproven
Independent chefs are often cash-strapped and skeptical of platforms. Solo-service-provider SaaS has notoriously high churn. If chefs love the free profile but won't pay for tools, the primary revenue stream collapses.
- **Mitigation:** validate concrete pricing in Phase 0; make the tools save *obvious* time/money; lean on the higher-value employer side as a revenue hedge.
- **Kill signal:** <~20–30% of engaged chefs convert to paid after a fair trial, with no path to fix it.

### 2. Cold-start on the directory (chicken-and-egg, softened but not gone)
An empty directory has no SEO value and no buyer value; no buyers means chefs see no inbound and don't stick.
- **Mitigation:** supply-first, concierge-seed 100+ chefs in one metro before opening to buyers; never display an empty directory; the off-platform-lead loop (`07`) seeds demand.
- **Kill signal:** can't get a single metro dense enough to generate organic inquiries within ~6 months of seeding.

### 3. SEO is slow and contested
The whole low-CAC demand thesis rests on ranking for `private chef [city]` — which agencies, existing platforms, and directories already target. It can take 6–18 months and may never reach page one in competitive metros.
- **Mitigation:** start SEO in Phase 2; combine with founder-led B2B sales and referrals so you're not SEO-dependent; pick a beachhead where competition is beatable.
- **Kill signal:** negligible organic traction after 12 months of real content + programmatic effort.

### 4. "Why pay you instead of Instagram + a free website?"
A chef's real alternative is free: Instagram for portfolio, a Squarespace site, Calendly, WhatsApp. Brigade must be clearly *more valuable than free fragments* — the differentiator is discovery (inbound leads) + trust (verification buyers recognize) + integrated workflow.
- **Mitigation:** the inbound-lead value and the buyer-recognized "verified" badge are things Instagram can't give. Lead with those.
- **Kill signal:** chefs use the free profile as a glorified link-in-bio and value nothing else.

### 5. Incumbent enters the niche
Culinary Agents or Hosco bolts on an "independent / private chef" mode and uses their existing audience to leapfrog supply.
- **Mitigation:** go deep where they're shallow (business tools + private-buyer marketplace they don't serve); own a geography densely; move faster than a big company will on a non-core segment; build switching costs via the tools layer.
- **Watch:** monitor their roadmaps.

### 6. Buyer side may want high-touch, not self-serve
The reason agencies charge 15–25% is that wealthy clients value *curation and accountability,* not a search box. Self-serve may under-serve the highest-value buyers.
- **Mitigation:** offer a "concierge/managed" tier on top of self-serve for premium buyers (best of both); verification + reviews substitute for some of the agency's trust role.
- **Open question:** is the buyer wedge affluent households (self-serve, SEO) or estates/agencies (relationship sales)? They want different products — pick the entry buyer deliberately (Phase 0).

### 7. Trust & liability, even without being merchant of record
You're publishing professional profiles and facilitating that strangers enter homes. A bad actor who got a "verified" badge and then harms a client is a reputational and possibly legal problem.
- **Mitigation:** real verification (Persona/Checkr), clear terms disclaiming you're not the employer/guarantor, insurance requirements, lawyer-reviewed ToS before launch.

### 8. Team / execution risk
Two-to-three-person team, one engineer, family member on design, non-technical CEO. Bandwidth is the constraint. Scope creep (building verticals or features early) is the most likely self-inflicted wound.
- **Mitigation:** ruthless scope discipline per `06`; don't expand verticals/geos until the core loop is proven; document the equity/roles so the team doesn't fracture.

## Kill-criteria (decide these *now*, while unemotional)

Pre-commit to walking away or pivoting if, after honest effort:
- Phase 0: fewer than ~40% of chefs will pay anything, **and** fewer than 3 buyers want paid access.
- Phase 5: can't get ~20 chefs to run a real booking through the tools.
- Phase 8: zero buyers will pay for hiring access after genuine sales effort.
Writing these down now prevents the sunk-cost spiral that kept doomed marketplaces alive too long.

## Open questions to resolve before Phase 3

1. **Which metro** is the beachhead, and why is it winnable?
2. **Which buyer** is the entry wedge — affluent households (SEO/self-serve) or estates/agencies (B2B sales)?
3. **Subscription price point** — validated, not guessed.
4. **Equity / vesting / IP / wife's role** — papered (see `08`).
5. **Name + domain + trademark** — cleared.
6. **Is anyone full-time?** If it's nights-and-weekends for everyone, the timeline in `06` roughly doubles — plan honestly.

## The honest bottom line

This is a *genuinely better* business than the commission marketplace: single-sided to start, non-leaking revenue, simpler legally, defensible via tools + reputation + network, and expandable across hospitality. It is **not** a slam dunk — chef willingness-to-pay and directory density are real, unproven bets. But they're *validatable cheaply in Phase 0 before you build*, which is exactly the discipline the graveyard companies lacked. Validate first. Then build.
