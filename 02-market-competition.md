# 02 — Market & Competition

## Market framing

The personal/private chef services market is real and growing (commonly cited around the low-single-digit $B range and expanding as at-home dining normalized post-2020). But for Brigade the relevant market is **not** "meals served" — it's:

1. **Software spend by independent hospitality professionals** (SaaS seats), and
2. **Hiring/placement spend for private hospitality talent** (currently flowing to agencies at 15–25% of salary).

That second pool is large and inefficient — every placement an agency makes by hand is a transaction a self-serve directory can capture a slice of at far lower cost.

## The graveyard (booking-marketplace cautionary tales)

These validate the *demand* and the *failure mode* we're avoiding:

- **Kitchit** (SF, 2011–2016): ~$8.1M raised, 100k+ meals, NPS 87 — beloved, still folded; market too small for venture scale at their cost structure.
- **Kitchensurfing** (NYC, ~2012–2016): ~$20M raised from Tiger Global, Spark, USV; tried advance-booking *and* on-demand; neither produced sustainable demand. Died the same week as Kitchit and Dinner Lab.
- **Lesson:** great product + lots of money still failed on marketplace economics + thin frequency. The on-demand "everyday chef" pivot specifically has a grave marker. **We are not running this play.**

## Survivors / proof the demand side works

- **Yhangry** (London): leaned into *one-off social dinner parties*, bundled grocery + chef-matching + menus into the workflow (a leakage defense), stayed lean (single-digit $M raised), grew city-by-city. Proof that focus + workflow-embedding works.
- **Take a Chef, Platesfull, Savour, etc.:** ongoing booking platforms — our *potential demand partners*, not direct competitors, since we're selling chef infrastructure, not competing to book the meal.

## Direct-ish competition (the network / directory / SaaS space)

| Competitor | What they are | Why they're not us / the gap |
|---|---|---|
| **Culinary Agents** | "LinkedIn for food & hospitality" — networking + job matching | Restaurant/hotel **W-2 jobs**, not the independent private chef's *business*. No private-client discovery, no business tools. |
| **Hosco** | 1.5M+ member hospitality network, jobs + courses | Same — career/employment oriented, global restaurant/hotel. Not private-chef-as-business. |
| **Poached, Sirvo, iHireChefs, OysterLink, ChefCrossing** | Niche hospitality job boards | Job boards for venues hiring staff. Not a profile/business/discovery product for solo chefs. |
| **Qwick** | On-demand hourly hospitality staffing | Shift-fill for venues. Different buyer, different job. |
| **Recruitment agencies** (Hospitalio, The Chef Agency, Chef Talent Agency) | High-touch human placement, incl. a "Private" category (private chefs, estate managers, butlers) | Expensive, manual, paid-on-hire. **This is exactly the incumbent we undercut with self-serve.** Proof the private-hire demand exists. |
| **Generic tools** (Instagram, personal Squarespace site, Calendly, QuickBooks, WhatsApp) | What chefs cobble together today | Fragmented, unprofessional, no discovery, no vetting/trust signal. **This fragmentation is our real competitor.** |

## The white space (one sentence)

> A **credibility + back-office + discovery** product purpose-built for the *independent private chef as a small business* — and the private/estate buyers who hire them — which none of the W-2 hospitality networks, job boards, or generic tools currently serve.

## Why now

- At-home private dining normalized and stuck post-2020.
- The "creator/solopreneur tooling" wave (think: what Honeybook did for event pros, Jane/Calendly for services) has trained independent professionals to pay for software that makes them look legit and saves admin time.
- Agencies are expensive and slow; buyers want self-serve.
- AI lets a 2–3 person team build menu generators, inquiry triage, proposal drafting, etc. cheaply — features that would've needed a big team before.

## Biggest competitive risk

**Culinary Agents (or Hosco) adds a "private chef / independent" mode.** They have the audience already. Our defense: go deep on the *business-tools + private-buyer* side they don't care about, own a geography densely, and move faster than an incumbent will on a non-core segment.
