# 03 — Business Model

## Core principle: monetize presence and access, never the meal

Every revenue stream below is chosen because **the user has no incentive to route around it.** A chef pays for their profile and tools regardless of where a given client came from. A buyer pays for access to vetted talent. Nobody can "cut out the platform" because the platform isn't standing between a chef and a client taking a cut of their dinner.

## Revenue streams (in build order)

### 1. Chef subscription (Freemium → Pro) — *primary, launches first*
- **Free tier:** basic public profile, listed in directory, limited inbound inquiries. Goal: maximum supply + SEO surface area.
- **Pro tier (~$15–39/mo, validate):** full business tools (calendar, quote builder, invoicing, CRM, menu builder), premium profile (more photos, video, custom URL, "verified" badge), priority directory placement, analytics.
- Why it works: the tools save real admin hours and make the chef look professional to *their own* clients. Honeybook/Calendly have proven solo service pros pay for this.

### 2. Featured / promoted placement — *the evolved "$5/day ad"*
- Chefs pay to rank higher in directory search for their city/cuisine, or to appear in "featured chefs" modules.
- Kai's original ad instinct, productized. Pure margin, no leakage.

### 3. Employer / hiring access (the LinkedIn-Recruiter analogue) — *highest-value, B2B*
- Estate managers, family offices, villa/yacht operators, catering agencies pay to: search/filter the vetted directory, post private gigs/roles, and contact chefs directly.
- Tiered: per-post, or recruiter-style monthly seat (~$99–499/mo, validate).
- **This is where LinkedIn makes its money, and it's the least leaky stream** — buyers hire repeatedly and value the vetting, not a one-off match.

### 4. Verification & trust pass-through — *trust as a product*
- Background-check, food-safety-cert, insurance verification — offered as paid "verified" badges (cost + margin over the underlying provider).
- Doubles as a trust signal that makes the directory worth more to buyers.

### 5. Future / opt-in (Phase 14+)
- **Optional payments rail:** chefs *choose* to invoice/collect deposits through Brigade because it adds value (deposit protection, escrow, auto-reminders, chargeback handling) — value-added, never a mandatory cut.
- **Fintech attach:** instant payout, income-smoothing, insurance, benefits for independent chefs.
- **Data/benchmarking:** anonymized rate/demand data sold to industry.
- **Academy/certification:** Brigade-branded credentialing (Hosco does courses; there's appetite).

## Why NOT booking commission (the thing we're deliberately refusing)

A 10%+10% commission is the highest-leakage revenue in this category and the reason the graveyard exists. If we ever add payments, it's opt-in and value-justified — the moment a chef feels the platform is a tax on a relationship they sourced themselves, they leave. We don't build that incentive in.

## Illustrative unit economics (to validate, not gospel)

These are **placeholder assumptions for Kai/Jordan to pressure-test**, not researched figures:

- Pro subscription: assume ~$25/mo → ~$300/yr per paying chef.
- If 30% of free chefs convert to Pro, and a metro has ~500 active chefs → ~150 Pro × $300 = ~$45k/yr from one city's subscriptions alone.
- Employer access likely *higher* total: even 40 paying buyers at ~$150/mo avg = ~$72k/yr per dense metro.
- Featured placement + verification: incremental margin on top.
- **Takeaway:** revenue is geography-multiplied. Saturate a city, then replicate. The model scales by *adding dense metros*, not by squeezing more out of each transaction.

> ⚠️ These are arithmetic illustrations to frame the model, not financial projections or advice. Real conversion rates, willingness-to-pay, and CAC must come from Phase 0 discovery and Phase 6 live data.

## What must be true for the model to work (assumptions to validate first)

1. Chefs will pay a monthly subscription for tools + credibility. *(Test in Phase 0 with real pricing conversations.)*
2. Enough buyers want self-serve vetted-chef access to pay for it. *(Test by pre-selling to estate managers/agencies before building the employer side.)*
3. We can make a single metro dense enough to be useful to buyers. *(The whole GTM in `07`.)*

If #1 or #2 is false, the model needs rework — see kill-criteria in `09`.
